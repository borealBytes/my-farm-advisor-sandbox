## The Reading Process

### Five Essential Components

1. **Phonemic Awareness** - Hearing sounds in words
2. **Phonics** - Sound-letter relationships
3. **Fluency** - Reading smoothly and quickly
4. **Vocabulary** - Understanding word meanings
5. **Comprehension** - Understanding text meaning

### The 7 Reading Strategies

**Strategy 1: Previewing**
- Look at title, headings, pictures
- Ask: What might this be about?
- Activate prior knowledge

**Strategy 2: Predicting**
- Guess what will happen next
- Check if predictions are correct
- Adjust understanding as needed

**Strategy 3: Visualizing**
- Create mental pictures
- Imagine characters, settings, actions
- Connect images to text

**Strategy 4: Making Connections**
- Text-to-self: How does this relate to my life?
- Text-to-text: How is this like other things I've read?
- Text-to-world: How does this connect to real life?

**Strategy 5: Questioning**
- Ask: Who? What? When? Where? Why? How?
- Question unclear parts
- Seek answers in the text

**Strategy 6: Inferring**
- Read between the lines
- Use clues to understand unstated meaning
- Combine text + background knowledge

**Strategy 7: Summarizing**
- Identify main ideas
- Put ideas in your own words
- Remember key points

## Leveled Reading Materials

### Level 1: Beginner (CEFR A1-A2)

**Characteristics:**
- 100-500 word texts
- Simple present tense
- Common vocabulary
- Clear topic sentences
- Pictures support text

**Sample Text: "My Morning Routine"**

I wake up at 6:00 every day. First, I wash my face and brush my teeth. Then, I eat breakfast. I usually have eggs and toast. After breakfast, I get dressed. I wear comfortable clothes for work. At 7:30, I leave my house. I drive to work. The drive takes 30 minutes. I arrive at 8:00. I say hello to my coworkers. Then I start my work.

**Comprehension Questions:**
1. What time does the person wake up? {{Q1_ANSWER}}
2. What does the person eat for breakfast? {{Q2_ANSWER}}
3. How does the person get to work? {{Q3_ANSWER}}
4. What time does the person arrive at work? {{Q4_ANSWER}}
5. Who does the person say hello to? {{Q5_ANSWER}}

**Vocabulary from Text:**
| Word | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| wake up | to stop sleeping | I wake up early |
| brush | to clean with a brush | Brush your teeth |
| usually | most of the time | I usually eat at 6 |
| comfortable | easy to wear, relaxed | These shoes are comfortable |
| coworkers | people you work with | My coworkers are friendly |

### Level 2: Intermediate (CEFR B1-B2)

**Characteristics:**
- 500-1000 word texts
- Multiple tenses
- Academic vocabulary
- Implicit main ideas
- Complex sentences

**Sample Text: "The Impact of Technology on Work"**

Technology has dramatically changed how people work. In the past, employees had to be in an office to do their jobs. Now, many people work from home. This is called "remote work" or "telecommuting."

Remote work has both advantages and disadvantages. One major benefit is flexibility. Workers can choose when and where they work. They don't waste time commuting. They can spend more time with family. Many people report being more productive at home.

However, remote work also has challenges. Some workers feel isolated. They miss talking to coworkers face-to-face. Work and personal life can become mixed together. It can be hard to "turn off" work at the end of the day.

Companies are still figuring out the best approach. Some have fully remote teams. Others require everyone to return to the office. Many are trying a "hybrid" model. In hybrid work, employees come to the office some days and work from home other days.

Experts believe the future of work will likely include more flexibility. Technology will continue to change how we communicate and collaborate. The key is finding balance between productivity and well-being.

**Comprehension Questions:**
1. What is another name for remote work? {{Q1_ANSWER}}
2. What are two benefits of remote work mentioned in the text? {{Q2_ANSWER}}
3. What challenge do some remote workers face? {{Q3_ANSWER}}
4. What is hybrid work? {{Q4_ANSWER}}
5. What do experts think about the future of work? {{Q5_ANSWER}}
6. What does the word "isolated" mean in paragraph 3? {{Q6_ANSWER}}
7. What is the main idea of this text? {{Q7_ANSWER}}

### Level 3: Advanced (CEFR C1-C2)

**Characteristics:**
- 1000+ word texts
- Complex academic language
- Abstract concepts
- Multiple viewpoints
- Nuanced arguments

**Sample Text: "The Paradox of Choice in Modern Society"**

In his influential 2004 book "The Paradox of Choice," psychologist Barry Schwartz argued that while freedom of choice is fundamental to human autonomy, having too many options can actually decrease happiness and satisfaction. This counterintuitive proposition challenges the assumption that more choice is always better.

Schwartz distinguishes between "maximizers" and "satisficers." Maximizers approach decisions with the goal of making the absolute best choice. They exhaustively research options, compare alternatives, and worry about missing out on better possibilities. Satisficers, in contrast, set standards for what is "good enough" and choose the first option that meets those criteria. Research suggests that while maximizers may achieve objectively better outcomes, satisficers report higher satisfaction with their choices and experience less regret.

The implications extend beyond individual psychology to questions of public policy and organizational design. Should governments restrict certain choices to protect citizens from decision fatigue? Should employers offer fewer healthcare plan options to reduce employee stress? These questions highlight the tension between liberal ideals of autonomy and emerging insights about cognitive limitations.

Critics have challenged aspects of Schwartz's framework. Some argue that cultural differences influence how people experience choice - what feels overwhelming in individualistic Western contexts may feel liberating in collectivist societies. Others suggest that choice overload primarily occurs with trivial decisions rather than important life choices, where people may actually benefit from extensive deliberation.

Nevertheless, the paradox of choice has gained significant traction in applied settings. Some technology companies have redesigned their interfaces to reduce option paralysis. Retailers have experimented with "curated choice" models that offer fewer, higher-quality options. These applications suggest that understanding the psychology of choice has practical value for design, marketing, and organizational behavior.

**Comprehension Questions:**
1. What is the "paradox of choice"? {{Q1_ANSWER}}
2. How do "maximizers" approach decisions differently from "satisficers"? {{Q2_ANSWER}}
3. Which group tends to be more satisfied with their choices? {{Q3_ANSWER}}
4. What policy questions does the paradox of choice raise? {{Q4_ANSWER}}
5. What criticisms have been made of Schwartz's framework? {{Q5_ANSWER}}
6. How have companies applied insights from this research? {{Q6_ANSWER}}
7. What is the author's overall perspective on the paradox of choice? {{Q7_ANSWER}}

## Vocabulary Building Strategies

### Strategy 1: Context Clues

Use the words around an unknown word to guess its meaning.

**Example:**
"The doctor prescribed antibiotics to treat the infection."

Clues: doctor, treat → medicine → **prescribed** = ordered/gave medicine

**Practice:**
"The weather was inclement, so we stayed inside."
{{CONTEXT_CLUE_1}}

### Strategy 2: Word Parts

Break words into roots, prefixes, and suffixes.

| Prefix | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| un- | not | unhappy = not happy |
| re- | again | rewrite = write again |
| pre- | before | preview = view before |
| dis- | opposite | disagree = not agree |

| Suffix | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| -tion | act/state | education = state of learning |
| -able | can be | readable = can be read |
| -ness | quality | happiness = quality of being happy |
| -ly | in manner | quickly = in quick manner |

### Strategy 3: Word Families

Learn words in groups.

**Happy family:**
- happy (adjective)
- happily (adverb)
- happiness (noun)
- unhappy (opposite)

**Help family:**
- help (verb/noun)
- helpful (adjective)
- helpless (adjective)
- helping (noun/verb form)

### Strategy 4: Personal Connection

Connect new words to your life.

"My grandmother is **frugal** - she saves money and doesn't waste anything."

## Comprehension Skills

### Main Idea vs. Details

**Main Idea:** What the whole text is about (the big picture)
**Details:** Specific information that supports the main idea

**Example:**
Text: "Dogs make good pets because they are loyal, protective, and loving."

### Summarizing

Use the "Somebody-Wanted-But-So" method:

**Somebody:** Who is the story about?
**Wanted:** What did they want?
**But:** What was the problem?
**So:** What happened?

**Example:**
Somebody: Maria
Wanted: to get to work on time
But: it was raining and she missed the bus
So: she took a taxi and arrived just in time
