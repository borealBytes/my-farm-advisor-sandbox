### 4.2 Response to § 102 Rejection (Anticipation)

**Rejection:** Claims [X-Y] stand rejected under 35 U.S.C. § 102(a)(1) as anticipated by [Reference] (US X,XXX,XXX).

**Applicant's Response:**

Applicant respectfully traverses this rejection.

[Reference] discloses [summary of reference teachings]. However, [Reference] fails to disclose [specific claim element(s) not taught by the reference].

Specifically, claim [X] recites "[quote the specific claim language]." [Reference], at [column X, lines Y-Z / paragraph XX], discloses [quote or paraphrase the reference]. This disclosure does not teach or suggest [the specific element], because [detailed technical explanation of why the reference does not anticipate].

[For each missing element, provide a detailed comparison between the claim language and the reference disclosure.]

Accordingly, Applicant respectfully submits that claim [X] is not anticipated by [Reference], and withdrawal of this rejection is respectfully requested.

### 4.3 Response to § 103 Rejection (Obviousness)

**Rejection:** Claims [X-Y] stand rejected under 35 U.S.C. § 103 as obvious over [Primary Reference] in view of [Secondary Reference].

**Applicant's Response:**

Applicant respectfully traverses this rejection.

To establish a prima facie case of obviousness, the Examiner must demonstrate: (1) that all claim elements are taught or suggested by the prior art; (2) that a person having ordinary skill in the art (PHOSITA) would have had a reason to combine the references; and (3) that the PHOSITA would have had a reasonable expectation of success. See _KSR Int'l Co. v. Teleflex Inc._, 550 U.S. 398 (2007); MPEP § 2143.

**Missing Element(s):**

[Identify claim elements not taught by the combination. For each missing element, explain why neither the primary nor secondary reference, alone or in combination, teaches or suggests it.]

**No Motivation to Combine:**

[Argue that a PHOSITA would not have been motivated to combine the references. Address whether the references are in analogous art, whether they teach away from the combination, and whether the combination would render either reference inoperable.]

**Teaching Away:**

[If applicable, argue that one or more references teach away from the claimed invention. Cite specific passages.]

**Secondary Considerations:**

[Present evidence of secondary considerations of non-obviousness, if available:]

- Commercial success of the claimed invention
- Long-felt but unsolved need
- Failure of others
- Unexpected results
- Skepticism of experts

Accordingly, Applicant respectfully submits that claims [X-Y] are not obvious over the cited combination, and withdrawal of this rejection is respectfully requested.

**§ 112(a) — Written Description / Enablement:**

The specification at [paragraph XX / column X, lines Y-Z] discloses [quoted passage], which provides written description support for the claim limitation "[limitation]."

**§ 112(b) — Indefiniteness:**

[Address each indefiniteness rejection. Either amend the claim to clarify the language or argue that the claim language is sufficiently definite when read in light of the specification.]

### 4.5 Response to § 101 Rejection (Subject Matter Eligibility)

**Rejection:** Claims [X-Y] stand rejected under 35 U.S.C. § 101 as directed to an abstract idea.

**Applicant's Response:**

Under the _Alice/Mayo_ framework (MPEP § 2106):

**Step 2A, Prong One:** [Argue that the claims are not directed to a judicial exception, or identify the specific alleged abstract idea.]

**Step 2A, Prong Two:** [Argue that the claims integrate any alleged judicial exception into a practical application. Identify the additional elements and explain how they impose meaningful limits. Cite relevant USPTO guidance and case law (e.g., *Enfish*, *DDR Holdings*, *McRO*).]

**Step 2B:** [In the alternative, argue that the claims recite significantly more than the abstract idea. Identify the inventive concept.]

The claims recite a specific technical improvement to [technical field], namely [description of improvement]. This is not an abstract idea, but rather a concrete technical solution that improves the functioning of [computer / technical system]. See _Enfish, LLC v. Microsoft Corp._, 822 F.3d 1327 (Fed. Cir. 2016).
