## The Communication Model

### The Communication Process

```
SENDER → ENCODING → MESSAGE → CHANNEL → DECODING → RECEIVER
                ↑                    ↑
            FEEDBACK ←←←←←←←←←←←← NOISE
```

**Key Elements:**
1. **Sender:** Person initiating communication
2. **Encoding:** Turning thoughts into message
3. **Message:** Information being conveyed
4. **Channel:** Medium (in-person, email, phone, etc.)
5. **Decoding:** Interpreting the message
6. **Receiver:** Person receiving communication
7. **Feedback:** Response confirming understanding
8. **Noise:** Barriers to clear communication

## Verbal Communication

### Active Listening

**The 5 Levels of Listening:**

**Level 1: Ignoring**
- Not paying attention at all
- Distracted by other things

**Level 2: Pretending**
- Nodding but not engaged
- Thinking about response

**Level 3: Selective**
- Hearing only parts
- Filtering for what you want to hear

**Level 4: Attentive**
- Paying full attention
- Focusing on the speaker

**Level 5: Empathetic**
- Understanding feelings
- Seeing from their perspective

**Active Listening Techniques:**

**SOLER Method:**
- **S** - Sit squarely (face the speaker)
- **O** - Open posture (no crossed arms)
- **L** - Lean slightly forward
- **E** - Eye contact (appropriate level)
- **R** - Relax (natural, not stiff)

**Verbal Affirmations:**
- "I see"
- "I understand"
- "Go on"
- "Tell me more"
- "That makes sense"

**Reflective Listening:**
- Paraphrase: "So what you're saying is..."
- Summarize: "Let me make sure I understand..."
- Clarify: "Do you mean...?"
- Reflect feelings: "It sounds like you're frustrated..."

### Speaking Clearly

**The 3V Model:**

**Verbal (Words):**
- Use clear, simple language
- Avoid jargon unless audience knows it
- Be specific, not vague
- Use concrete examples

**Vocal (Voice):**
- Volume: Loud enough to hear
- Pace: Not too fast or slow
- Pitch: Varies for emphasis
- Pause: Strategic breaks

**Visual (Body Language):**
- Eye contact
- Facial expressions
- Gestures
- Posture

**Speaking Tips:**

**Before Speaking:**
- Think before you speak
- Consider your audience
- Know your key points
- Anticipate questions

**While Speaking:**
- Start with main point
- Use "I" statements ("I think..." not "You always...")
- Be specific
- Check for understanding

**P-B-C Framework:**
- **P**oint: State your main point
- **B**ackup: Provide evidence/examples
- **C**all to action: What should happen next?

**Example:**
"I think we should change our approach to customer onboarding [POINT]. Currently, 30% of new customers don't complete setup, and surveys show they find it confusing [BACKUP]. I'd like us to redesign the first-time user experience by next month [CALL TO ACTION]."

### Asking Questions

**Types of Questions:**

**Open-Ended:**
- Cannot be answered yes/no
- Encourages discussion
- "What do you think about...?"
- "How would you approach...?"
- "Tell me about..."

**Closed-Ended:**
- Can be answered yes/no
- Gets specific information
- "Did you finish the report?"
- "Is the meeting at 3pm?"
- "Have you talked to them?"

**Probing:**
- Dig deeper
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "What happened next?"
- "Why do you think that is?"

**Clarifying:**
- Confirm understanding
- "Do you mean...?"
- "Are you saying...?"
- "Let me make sure I understand..."

**Question Starters:**

| Type | Starters |
|------|----------|
| Clarification | "Can you explain...", "What do you mean by..." |
| Exploration | "Tell me about...", "How did you..." |
| Comparison | "How is this different from...", "What are the options..." |
| Action | "What would you like me to do...", "What's the next step..." |

**Questions to Ask Your Manager:**
- What are your top priorities for the team?
- How do you prefer to receive updates?
- What's the best way to communicate with you?
- What would you like me to focus on this week?

**Questions to Ask Colleagues:**
- What's your experience with...?
- How do you approach...?
- Could you help me understand...?
- What would you recommend?

### Giving Feedback

**The SBI Model:**

**S**ituation: When and where did this occur?
**B**ehavior: What specifically did you observe?
**I**mpact: How did it affect you/others?

**Positive Feedback Example:**
"During yesterday's client presentation [Situation], you handled their difficult questions calmly and provided clear, helpful answers [Behavior]. The client told me afterward they felt confident in our team because of your expertise [Impact]."

**Constructive Feedback Example:**
"In this morning's team meeting [Situation], I noticed you interrupted Sarah twice while she was presenting [Behavior]. I think she may have felt her ideas weren't being heard, and we might have missed out on her input [Impact]. Next time, could you wait for her to finish before jumping in?"

**Feedback Best Practices:**
- Give feedback close to when it happened
- Be specific, not general
- Focus on behavior, not personality
- Balance positive and constructive
- Ask for their perspective
- Collaborate on solutions

### Receiving Feedback

**The GIFT Approach:**

**G**rateful: Thank them
**I**nquire: Ask questions to understand
**F**ocus: Listen without defending
**T**ake action: Make a plan

**Response Framework:**
1. Listen without interrupting
2. Take notes
3. Ask clarifying questions
4. Summarize what you heard
5. Thank them
6. Reflect before responding
7. Create action plan

**Example Response:**
"Thank you for sharing this with me. I want to make sure I understand - you're saying that in client meetings, I should give others more time to speak before I offer my perspective? That makes sense. I'll work on pausing before jumping in."

## Non-Verbal Communication

### Body Language

**Powerful Positions:**
- Stand/sit up straight
- Take up space
- Open posture (uncrossed arms)
- Firm handshake
- Steady eye contact
- Confident gestures

**Submissive Positions:**
- Slouching
- Making yourself small
- Crossed arms/legs
- Weak handshake
- Looking down
- Fidgeting

**Eye Contact:**
- Maintain 60-70% of time
- Break occasionally (don't stare)
- Different cultures, different norms
- Look at forehead if eye contact uncomfortable

**Facial Expressions:**
- Smile naturally (not forced)
- Match expression to message
- Avoid scowling or frowning
- Show engagement

**Gestures:**
- Use for emphasis
- Keep in "power zone" (chest to waist)
- Avoid pointing
- Don't fidget with objects
- Natural hand movements

### Personal Space

**Distance Zones:**

**Intimate (0-18 inches):**
- Close friends, family
- Romantic partners
- Not appropriate for work

**Personal (18 inches - 4 feet):**
- Conversations with friends
- Casual workplace interactions
- Comfortable for most

**Social (4-12 feet):**
- Business meetings
- Professional interactions
- Presentations

**Public (12+ feet):**
- Speaking to groups
- Large meetings
- Public events

### Appearance

**Professional Dress:**
- Know your industry norms
- When in doubt, dress slightly more formal
- Clothes should fit well
- Groomed and clean
- Minimal distractions (strong scents, noisy jewelry)

**Virtual Presence:**
- Good lighting on face
- Neutral or professional background
- Eye level camera
- Dress professionally (even bottom half!)
- Minimize distractions

## Written Communication

### Email Communication

**Email Structure:**

**Subject Line:**
- Clear and specific
- Include action needed
- Examples:
  - "Action Required: Review Q3 Budget by Friday"
  - "Question: Client Meeting on Thursday?"
  - "Update: Project X Status"

**Greeting:**
- Formal: "Dear Mr. Smith,"
- Standard: "Hi Sarah," or "Hello Team,"
- To group: "Hello everyone,"

**Opening:**
- State purpose clearly
- "I'm writing to..."
- "I wanted to update you on..."

**Body:**
- One main point per paragraph
- Use bullet points for lists
- Keep it concise
- Bold key information

**Closing:**
- Clear next steps
- "Please let me know by..."
- "Looking forward to..."

**Sign-off:**
- "Best regards,"
- "Sincerely,"
- "Thanks,"
- "Best,"

**Email Template:**

{{OPENING - State purpose}}

- {{ACTION_1}}

{{YOUR_NAME}}
{{YOUR_TITLE}}
{{CONTACT_INFO}}

### Email Best Practices

**DO:**
- ✅ Use clear subject lines
- ✅ Proofread before sending
- ✅ Respond within 24-48 hours
- ✅ Use "Reply All" sparingly
- ✅ Keep emails concise
- ✅ Use professional tone
- ✅ Include signature with contact info

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Use ALL CAPS (shouting)
- ❌ Send when emotional
- ❌ Include confidential info without encryption
- ❌ Use slang or emojis (in formal contexts)
- ❌ Forward chain emails
- ❌ Forget attachments
- ❌ Use "Reply All" for sensitive topics

**Common Email Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Better Approach |
|---------|-----------------|
| Vague subject | "Meeting" → "Marketing Team Meeting - March 15 at 2pm" |
| No clear action | Make action items explicit |
| Too long | Break into multiple emails or use attachments |
| Forgetting attachment | Check before sending |
| Replying to all unnecessarily | Consider if everyone needs to see it |

### Business Writing

**Clear Writing Principles:**

**1. Know Your Audience:**
- What do they already know?
- What do they need to know?
- What's their communication style?

**2. Start with the Bottom Line:**
- Put main point first
- Then provide details
- Don't bury the lead

**3. Be Concise:**
- Use short sentences
- Avoid unnecessary words
- Delete redundancies

**4. Use Active Voice:**
- Active: "The manager approved the budget."
- Passive: "The budget was approved by the manager."

**5. Be Specific:**
- Vague: "We need to improve sales."
- Specific: "We need to increase Q3 sales by 15% through new customer acquisition."

**The BLUF Method (Bottom Line Up Front):**

**Before:**
"Over the past few months, our team has been working on various initiatives to improve our customer service process. We've analyzed current workflows, identified pain points, and implemented several changes. I'm pleased to share that these efforts have resulted in significant improvements..."

**After (BLUF):**
"Customer satisfaction scores increased 25% following our service improvements. Details below..."

## Interpersonal Communication

### Building Relationships

**The Relationship Bank Account:**
- Every positive interaction = deposit
- Every negative interaction = withdrawal
- Keep balance positive

**Ways to Build Rapport:**
- Remember names and details
- Show genuine interest
- Find common ground
- Be reliable
- Offer help
- Give credit
- Say thank you

### Difficult Conversations

**The DESC Model:**

**D**escribe: Facts of the situation
**E**xpress: Your feelings/reactions
**S**pecify: What you want
**C**onsequences: What will happen

**Example:**
"When the report was submitted 2 days late [Describe], I felt stressed about meeting our client deadline [Express]. In the future, please give me 24 hours notice if you anticipate delays [Specify]. This way we can adjust expectations with the client [Consequence]."

**De-escalation Techniques:**
- Stay calm
- Use "I" statements
- Acknowledge their feelings
- Don't interrupt
- Focus on solution
- Offer choices

### Communication Across Cultures

**High-Context Cultures:**
- Meaning from context, relationship, non-verbals
- Examples: Japan, China, Korea, Arab countries
- Build relationship first
- Indirect communication
- Silence is okay

**Low-Context Cultures:**
- Meaning from explicit words
- Examples: USA, Germany, Scandinavia
- Get to business quickly
- Direct communication
- Silence uncomfortable

**Cross-Cultural Tips:**
- Research cultural norms
- Ask about preferences
- Be respectful
- Avoid assumptions
- Clarify understanding
- Be patient

### Communication Technology

**Video Calls:**
- Test technology beforehand
- Look at camera, not screen
- Mute when not speaking
- Use chat appropriately
- Have agenda
- Follow up with notes

**Instant Messaging:**
- Know platform norms (Slack vs Teams vs WhatsApp)
- Consider urgency vs importance
- Don't expect immediate response
- Use status indicators
- Keep it professional

**Phone Calls:**
- Identify yourself
- Ask if they have time
- Have agenda
- Take notes
- Summarize action items
- Send follow-up email

## Communication Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Giving Instructions

**Poor:** "I need that report soon."

**Better:** "I need the quarterly sales report by end of day Friday. Please include:
- Revenue by region
- Top 10 customers
- Year-over-year comparison
- Forecast for next quarter"

**Even Better:** "I'd like the quarterly sales report by end of day Friday [When]. This should include [What]:
- Revenue by region
- Top 10 customers
- Year-over-year comparison
- Forecast for next quarter

Please let me know if you have questions [Check for understanding]. I'll review it Monday morning and discuss with you Tuesday [Why/What's next]."

### Scenario 2: Saying No

**Direct but polite:**
"I appreciate you thinking of me, but I don't have capacity to take on additional projects this month. I'd be happy to help next month or recommend someone else."

**Offering alternative:**
"I can't take the lead on this, but I'd be happy to review the plan and provide feedback."

**Explaining priority:**
"I'm currently focused on finishing Project X by Friday. I could help with this starting next week. Would that work?"

### Scenario 3: Asking for Help

**Good approach:**
"I'm working on [project] and I'm stuck on [specific issue]. I've tried [approaches], but I'm not making progress. Would you have time to brainstorm with me for 15 minutes this afternoon?"

### Scenario 4: Delivering Bad News

**Framework:**
1. Prepare them: "I have some difficult news to share..."
2. State facts clearly
3. Explain impact
4. Discuss options/next steps

**Example:**
"I need to let you know that we won't hit the original deadline for Project X. We encountered unexpected technical challenges that added 2 weeks to our timeline. This means the client presentation will need to be moved to March 15. I've prepared a few options for how to communicate this to the client..."
